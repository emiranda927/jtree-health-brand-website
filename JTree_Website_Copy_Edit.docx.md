2. Page-by-Page Edits — specific before/after changes for each page, in the order a visitor would encounter them.

# **Part One: Strategic Findings**

These are the macro-level issues. Each one affects the site as a whole, not just a single page. They reveal where the brand's voice is drifting, where key messaging is missing, and where structural inconsistencies are undermining trust.

## **SF1 · "World-class" contradicts the brand's own values**

The footer tagline on the Programs and About pages reads: "World-class behavioral health for adolescents and families in North Carolina." The About page, in the same session, explicitly states: "No corporate gloss. No clinical distance." These two sentences cannot coexist.

"World-class" is exactly the kind of marketing language that erodes trust in healthcare. It signals that the brand is performing credibility rather than earning it. It is the kind of line that a frightened parent skims and discounts. Worse, it conflicts directly with Gaby's origin story, which is the brand's most powerful asset.

**The homepage footer tagline gets this right: "Named for a desert plant — and for a brother we lost too soon." That is the voice. Use it everywhere.**

Recommendation: Replace "World-class behavioral health for adolescents and families in North Carolina" across all footer instances with the homepage version, or a unified line that carries the same earned authority without the puffery.

| 📍 Footer tagline — Programs page, About page |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **BEFORE** | *World-class behavioral health for adolescents and families in North Carolina.* |
| **AFTER** | **Adolescent PHP & IOP in Apex, NC. Named for a desert plant — and for a brother we lost too soon.** |
| ↳ *Unified footer tagline. The specific, human version. Use this on every page.* |  |

## **SF2 · CTA language is fragmented across the site**

There are five different primary CTA phrasings in active use across the site:

* "Start Now" — nav bar

* "Start the Process" — homepage hero, homepage footer CTA

* "Start the Admissions Process" — admissions page hero

* "Start Admissions →" — programs page CTA buttons

* "Request a Callback →" — admissions form submit button

Each variation sends a slightly different signal. "Start Now" implies immediacy but no warmth. "Start the Process" implies bureaucracy. "Request a Callback" is accurate but clinical. None of them feel like the brand voice.

The brand kit recommended "Start the Conversation" as the primary CTA — this language is conspicuously absent from the current site. It reduces perceived commitment, matches the emotional register of a family in crisis, and accurately describes what the first step actually is: a phone call, not an enrollment.

Recommendation: Standardize the primary CTA as "Start the Conversation" across nav, hero sections, and footer CTAs. Reserve "Request a Callback" for the form submit button where it is functionally accurate. "Start Now" in the nav is acceptable as a compact version.

* **Primary CTA (full):**  "Start the Conversation"

* **Primary CTA (compact, nav):**  "Get Started" or keep "Start Now"

* **Form submit:**  "Request a Callback →"  (keep as-is — accurate here)

* **Secondary CTA:**  "Call Us" or "Talk to Our Team"

## **SF3 · The 3-step process is described differently on two pages**

The homepage has a "How It Works" section with steps titled: Fill out the form → We call you → Start within the week.

The admissions page has a "How It Works" section with steps titled: Submit this form → We call you back → Free intake assessment.

These are describing the same process. The steps are functionally identical but the language differs. This creates low-grade confusion: a parent who has read the homepage and then arrives at the admissions page sees a slightly different version of the same thing, which undermines clarity and trust.

Recommendation: Unify the three step titles and descriptions across both pages. The homepage version is stronger. Keep: "Fill out the form" → "We call you" → "Start within the week." Make the admissions page match.

| **AFTER** | **We call you** |

## **SF4 · "Built different. On purpose." is the wrong register**

"Built different" is Gen Z vernacular. It may resonate with the teen audience, but the Programs section on the homepage where this appears is primarily a conversion section for parents making a healthcare decision. Using generational slang in that context signals that the brand is trying to sound young rather than be trustworthy.

The brand is warm and grounded — it uses plain direct language, not borrowed cool. There is a difference between a brand that respects teens and a brand that performs youthfulness.

| 📍 Homepage — Programs section header |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **BEFORE** | *Built different.On purpose.* |
| **AFTER** | **Two programs. One team. Zero waitlists.** |
| ↳ *Functional, specific, surprising. Each clause does a job. This replaces a vague attitude with three concrete reasons to care. The subhead ("Two levels of outpatient care, one clinical team, zero waitlists.") can be cut or absorbed into the header.* |  |

## **SF5 · "The numbers speak." is an incomplete sentence**

The Outcomes section header on the homepage reads: "The numbers speak." This is meant to sound bold, but it is syntactically incomplete. It leaves a gap where the copy should close. What do the numbers say? The reader is asked to complete the thought themselves, at the moment when the brand should be doing the work.

The numbers that follow are strong: 82% symptom improvement, 78% improved family communication. Let the data drive the header, not a vague idiom.

| 📍 Homepage — Outcomes section header |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **BEFORE** | *The numbers speak.* |
| **AFTER** | **82% of teens. 78% of families.** |
| ↳ *Turn the stat block labels into the headline. This is earned, specific, and impossible to dismiss. The reader immediately knows what the section is claiming before they read the details.* |  |

## **SF6 · "You're in good company" is ambiguous**

The Referrers section header on the homepage is: "You're in good company." Who is "you" here? The parents reading the page? The providers? The sentence is trying to build credibility by naming trusted referral partners, but the headline doesn't do that job. A parent reading this may wonder whether they are the company, or whether the brand is complimenting itself.

The subhead is better: "Pediatricians, school counselors, and hospitals across the Triangle trust JTree Health." Lead with that idea.

| 📍 Homepage — Referrers section header |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **BEFORE** | *You're in good company.* |
| **AFTER** | **Trusted by providers across the Triangle.** |
| ↳ *Specific, clear, and the subject is JTree Health — not the reader. The credibility transfer is immediate.* |  |

| 📍 Homepage — Referrers section subhead |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **BEFORE** | *Pediatricians, school counselors, and hospitals across the Triangle trust JTree Health.* |
| **AFTER** | **Pediatricians, school counselors, and hospital discharge teams across Raleigh, Cary, Durham, and Chapel Hill refer their families to us.** |
| ↳ *More specific geography. "Refer their families to us" is more concrete and action-oriented than "trust." It implies an active ongoing relationship rather than a passive endorsement.* |  |

## **SF7 · Values on the About page conflict with the brand kit**

The About page lists three values: Grounded, Genuine, Growth-focused. The company VTO lists five: Accountability, Growth, Outcomes/Results, Compassion, Excellence. Neither set has been reconciled into a coherent public-facing statement of what JTree Health stands for.

The three G's (Grounded, Genuine, Growth-focused) are a reasonable distillation for public-facing brand language, but the value card descriptions undercut the header. The header promises "Three things we do that most programs don't" — but the cards describe traits and beliefs, not behaviors.

Recommendation: Keep the three G's as the public-facing values. Fix the section header to match what the cards actually deliver.

| 📍 About page — Values section header |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **BEFORE** | *Three things we do that most programs don't.* |
| **AFTER** | **Three commitments we make to every family.** |
| ↳ *The cards describe commitments (honest assessments, no corporate gloss, measurable growth). This header matches what the cards actually say, and "commitments" is a stronger trust signal than "things we do."* |  |

## **SF8 · The insurance note is worded inconsistently across PHP and IOP**

PHP section note: "PHP is covered by most major insurance plans. We are in-network with BCBS, Cigna, Aetna, UHC, and Tricare. We handle verification for you — no out-of-pocket surprises."

IOP section note: "IOP is covered by most major insurance plans. Same in-network carriers as PHP. We verify benefits before you start."

These say the same thing in different ways. "No out-of-pocket surprises" is slightly clunky. "We verify benefits before you start" is cleaner. The IOP version lazily refers back to PHP ("Same in-network carriers as PHP"), which is confusing if someone reads the IOP section first. Both should be fully self-contained.

Standardized insurance note (use on both PHP and IOP sections):

| 📍 Programs page — Insurance note (both PHP and IOP) |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **BEFORE** | *PHP/IOP is covered by most major insurance plans. We are in-network with BCBS, Cigna, Aetna, UHC, and Tricare. We handle verification for you — no out-of-pocket surprises. / Same in-network carriers as PHP. We verify benefits before you start.* |
| **AFTER** | **Covered by most major insurance plans. We're in-network with BCBS, Cigna, Aetna, UHC, and Tricare — and we verify your benefits before you start, so there are no surprises.** |
| ↳ *One clean sentence. Fully self-contained. Works for either program. "So there are no surprises" reads more naturally than "no out-of-pocket surprises."* |  |
